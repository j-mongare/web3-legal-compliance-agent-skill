# Reves Test Quick Reference Checklist
(Reves v. Ernst & Young, 494 U.S. 56 (1990))

## Overview
The Reves “family resemblance” test determines whether a note (or note-like instrument) is a security.  
**Key principle**: Notes are **presumed to be securities** unless they bear a strong “family resemblance” to instruments that are **not** securities (e.g., mortgage loans, consumer loans, short-term commercial paper, accounts receivable financing, or bank loans for current operations).

Courts examine **four factors**. All four should be weighed together.

## 1. Motivations of Seller & Buyer
- Does the **seller** issue the note to raise capital for its general business or substantial investments?  
- Does the **buyer** purchase primarily to earn a profit (interest, yield, return)?  
**Investment purpose** → leans strongly toward a security.  
**Commercial or consumer purpose** (e.g., short-term operational loan) → leans toward non-security.

## 2. Plan of Distribution
- Is the note offered and sold to a **broad segment of the public**?  
- Is there **common trading** for speculation or investment?  
- Is the offering **narrow/private** (e.g., limited to sophisticated parties with high minimums)?  
Wide public distribution → leans toward a security.

## 3. Reasonable Expectations of the Investing Public
- How is the instrument **marketed or advertised**?  
- Would a reasonable investor view it as an **investment** (e.g., yield, passive return, profit potential)?  
- Are there clear disclaimers that it is merely a loan or commercial instrument?  
Public perception as an investment → leans toward a security.

## 4. Risk-Reducing Factors / Alternative Regulatory Scheme
- Is there **collateral**, insurance, or other protections?  
- Does another regulatory regime (banking laws, commodities regulation, etc.) already significantly reduce investor risk?  
- Would applying securities laws be **unnecessary** because of existing protections?  
Strong risk-reducing factors → leans toward non-security.

## Instructions for Use in Analysis
- For each factor, explicitly document **evidence supporting** and **evidence weakening** the classification as a security.  
- Always note factual gaps or missing documentation.  
- Never reach a definitive “is/is not a security” conclusion — provide a balanced, preliminary assessment only.  
- In Web3/DeFi context, apply to yield-bearing tokens, stablecoin structures, lending protocols, or any instrument denominated as a “note,” “bond,” or debt-like product.

**Primary Source**: Reves v. Ernst & Young, 494 U.S. 56 (1990)  
**Application Note**: In crypto cases, courts and the SEC have applied Reves to instruments promising fixed or variable returns (e.g., interest-bearing tokens).

**Always cross-reference with Howey Test** when the instrument has both debt-like and equity-like features.
